# Book 1 — Decision Library Template

You're building a small library of Decisions — real publishing choices
from your own books — and telling me which parts of the product each
one applies to. One good Decision about narrowing a vague reader can
correctly cover the Discover "Reader" question, the Discover "Promise"
question, AND a Refine "Reader Focus" finding — write it once, map it
several times.

Called "Decisions," not "lessons" or "case studies": the whole product
asks one question, over and over — *"would an experienced publisher make
the same decision?"* The evidence backing each review should be framed
the same way. The software literally says **"Why I made this
decision"** when it surfaces one — that's the line everything else is
built around. ("Decision" is the internal architecture name — the
library, the IDs — but the heading a writer actually sees is more
personal: "Why I made this decision," not "Related publishing decision.")

**The five parts, every time:**
1. **The Situation** — what the book originally looked like.
2. **The Discovery** — how you realized something was wrong. (be concrete — a beta reader's specific comment, a re-read, a pattern you spotted)
3. **The Change** — exactly what you decided to do about it.
4. **The Outcome** — what improved afterward.
5. **The Principle** — the one memorable, reusable takeaway. This is what a writer remembers and repeats to someone else — spend real care on this line.

After each Decision, tell me where it applies — pick from the
Application Points list below. Most good Decisions apply to 2-3 places.

## Application Points

**Discover phase:**
- `discover.concept` — "Describe your book in one sentence"
- `discover.reader` — "Who do you want this book to help?"
- `discover.promise` — "What changes for them?"
- `discover.positioning` — "Where would this sit on a shelf?"

**Design phase:**
- `design.spineStatement` — building the narrative spine (weak stakes, vague action)
- `design.spineStressTest` — a spine that didn't survive a swap
- `design.chapterSpineCheck` — a chapter that didn't earn its place in the outline

**Package phase:**
- `package.titleCandidates` — choosing between real title options
- `package.subtitleBuilder` — naming the objection, not just the benefit
- `package.packageReview` — a title that drifted from the original promise

**Publish phase:**
- `publish.descriptionCopy` — sales copy discipline (e.g. bio longer than description)

**Refine phase (manuscript review findings):**
- `refine.bookPromiseAlignment` — book doesn't deliver its title's promise
- `refine.chapterOverlap` — two chapters cover the same ground
- `refine.readerFocus` — manuscript addresses multiple audiences inconsistently
- `refine.filterWords` — "I felt / I noticed / I realized" overused
- `refine.introPromise` — introduction doesn't state the promise early
- `refine.conclusionFulfillment` — conclusion doesn't return to the promise
- `refine.absoluteLanguage` — "always / never / every" overused
- `refine.pacingVariance` — one chapter's rhythm is a sharp outlier
- `refine.commercialReview` — title/subtitle reads as too abstract

*(`chapterLengthBalance` and `sentenceParagraphRhythm` have no Decision
slot, by design — too mechanical/generic for a real decision story to
add value there.)*

## A worked example, so the shape is clear

### Decision — Narrowing a vague reader

**Book:** The Invisible Trap

**The Situation:** My first draft said this book was for "anyone who
struggles with self-doubt" — which is everyone, which means it was
really for no one.

**The Discovery:** A beta reader told me she liked it but couldn't tell
me who it was for in one sentence. I realized I couldn't answer that
either.

**The Change:** I narrowed the reader to mid-career professionals who
got passed over for a promotion and started questioning their own
competence.

**The Outcome:** Every chapter got sharper because I could write toward
one specific person's situation instead of a vague feeling everyone has
sometimes.

**The Principle:** If you can't describe your reader in one sentence,
your chapters will try to speak to everyone and satisfy no one.

**Applies to:** `discover.reader`, `discover.promise`, `refine.readerFocus`

## Suggested starting point

Three or four well-told Decisions, each mapped to 2-3 application
points, covers more real ground than ten thin single-use ones. Start
wherever the story comes easiest. IDs (`decision-001`, `decision-002`...)
are assigned in the order you send them, not based on which feature
they happen to apply to first — a decision can always be mapped to a
newly-discovered relevant spot later without rewriting anything.
